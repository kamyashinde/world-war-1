INTRODUCTION

The First World War, otherwise known as the Great War lasted from 1914 to 1918. The war was fought by the Triple Alliance against the Triple Entente. The commonly known reason for the start of WW1 is said to be the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria, but many believe that there were multiple events and conflicts that eventually led up to the start of the war. It was as if the assassination was the straw that broke the camel's back. This war was supposed to last until Christmas 1914 but ended up stretching. Soon, the United States of America was dragged into the conflict. The world saw millions of deaths before the countries signed the peace treaty - the Treaty of Versailles in 1919. The treaty had a huge impact on the defeated Germany, which contributed to the start of the Second World War.

ALLIANCES

Alliances, according to Oxford dictionaries mean a union or association formed for mutual benefit, especially between countries or organizations. During the first world war, most of the major powers were set against each other. The two alliances were the Triple Alliance, consisting of Austria-Hungary, Italy and Germany, and the Triple Entente, consisting of Great Britain, France and Russia. In 1873, Russia, Austria-Hungary and Germany formed an alliance. But, in 1878, Russia withdrew from the alliance and the dual alliance was formed (Germany and Austria-Hungary). This dual alliance was later joined by Italy to form a Triple Alliance in 1882. During WW1, Italy ended up changing sides and supported the Triple Entente. The reason why the Triple Entente was formed was due to concerns about the rising threat from the rival alliance. (Wilde)

The First Morrocan Crisis
In 1905, France was looking to expand its colonies by making Morroco a part of the French colony. At that time, Morocco was one of the few parts that were not occupied by European powers. But, Wilhelm II of Germany did not want this to happen. Therefore, he visited Morroco and announced that he supports a free Morroco. This increased tensions between France and Germany, but in the end, France got special rights on Morroco after conducting a conference between the countries. Although the conference temporarily solved the First Morrocan Crisis, it only worsened the tensions between the Triple Alliance and the Triple Entente that ultimately led to the First World War. (Soroka)

Germany vs France and Great Britain

After the Morrocan Crisis, Germany was not happy with France gaining special rights over Morroco. Therefore, in 1911, Kaiser Wilhelm interfered with Morroco by sending rebels to attack a town. Since France had rights over Morroco, they sent aid to help with the attack. But, Wilhelm eventually accused France of the attack, due to which war preparations were made. At this time, Britain got involved and supported France since they were threatened by the Naval Law in Germany. Even though Germany backed down and the war was prevented, these events created more tension between Germany and France, as well as Germany and Great Britain.

Balkan Wars

Serbia wanted it to become completely Slavic, and their main ally, Russia, supported them. But, Austria-Hungary did not want this to happen. This was due to the fact that they already had a large Slavic population, and if they let it happen, the population would rebel if Serbia was all Slavs. This led to the annexation of the two countries Serbia wanted, Bosnia and Herzegovina, by Austria-Hungary. This deeply outraged Serbia and the tensions rose steadily between them for several years. In fact, Austria-Hungary wanted to crush Serbia due to its increased size after the war. (Allan)

TREATY OF VERSAILLES

After the countries declared war on each other, it stretched until 1919. In the end, Germany was defeated and the main countries that were victorious were France and Britain. The leaders of Britain, France and the USA arrived at the conference in Paris, January 1919 to sign the peace treaty in Versailles. They felt pressured to punish Germany since the victorious countries felt strongly that Germany was responsible for the war. During the conference, there was disagreement as to what the main aim of the conference was (Walsh). Some felt that the aim was to punish Germany, others felt that the aim was to cripple Germany so that it could not start another war, many felt that the point of the Conference was to reward the winning countries while some others believed that the aim of the conference should be to establish a just and lasting peace. (Walsh) In the end, the conference was ended with the creation of fourteen points that are shown in the picture to the right. Since the Treaty held Germany accountable for the First World War, it is safe to say that the treaty of Versailles played a big role in starting the Second World War.
